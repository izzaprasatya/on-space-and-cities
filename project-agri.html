Agricultural Geography

Agriculture in the Vicinity of Rural Settlements

Year 2023
Tools QGIS 3.28, Felt
Methods Remote Sensing, Zonal Statistics, Map Algebra
Location Balıkesir–Bursa, Türkiye
Data Sources Sentinel-2

GIS & Remote Sensing reflections from a lowland plain in Marmara, Türkiye

GIS and Remote Sensing are two approaches that have proven themselves quietly useful within the practice of agriculture. One of their many gifts is the ability to read the land without walking every cm of it, and to translate broad landscapes into measurable conclusions of vegetation, moisture, and water signals.

In this short discourse, I examine the agricultural health of rural settlements surrounding Lake Manyas: a constellation of villages that administratively belongs to Bursa Province and Balıkesir Province, both situated in the Marmara Region, in the northwestern edge of Türkiye.

Agriculture around Lake Manyas is widely shaped by its setting: a lowland plain with fertile, water-influenced soils, where farming tends to be productive and widespread. The lake itself is shallow freshwater, and its seasonal wet–dry cycles often imprint themselves onto the surrounding fields. In general, the agricultural character here is commonly associated with cereals such as wheat and barley, and oilseeds such as sunflower, with certain patches of land also influenced by wetland-edge moisture dynamics.

Methodology

To interpret agricultural condition across settlements, I employed a geographically simple spatial workflow:

  • 1) Rural settlements were mapped as points.
  • 2) A 900-meter buffer was created for each settlement to represent the nearby agricultural landscape within a short rural radius.
  • 3) For each buffer, I extracted zonal statistics from three indices derived from satellite imagery:
    • NDVI — vegetation vigor / greenness
    • NDMI — canopy or surface moisture condition
    • NDWI — water signal (wetter surfaces typically move closer to 0, sometimes positive)

In short: NDVI tells how alive the vegetation appears, while NDMI tells me how moist it remains, and NDWI hints whether water bodies / wet surfaces are influencing the scene.

Index reminder: High NDVI does not always mean “best agriculture”. It can also represent dense natural vegetation (forest) inside the buffer. Thus, NDVI must be read together with NDMI and NDWI.

Findings

Within the 900 m buffers, a settlement that stands out as one of the most convincing representations of healthy agricultural condition is Yenisarıbey, with avg NDVI = 0.39 and avg NDMI = 0.30. These values indicate strong vegetation vigor accompanied by sufficient canopy moisture, without the buffer being overly dominated by water-surface signal.

Meanwhile, the highest NDVI values belong to Doğancı (0.46) and Ömerler (0.42), yet these should be interpreted with care. Their greenness can be influenced by the dominance of natural vegetation (forest) within the buffer, meaning the indices may represent summer vitality of natural cover rather than purely agricultural productivity.

One notable outlier is Gölyaka, where avg NDVI = 0.09 but avg NDMI = 0.46 and avg NDWI = -0.07. This suggests a landscape with a strong wet surface influence (wetland edge / proximity to water) where moisture is high, yet vegetation vigor is limited.

Closing reflection

To read agriculture around Lake Manyas is to read a landscape negotiated by water. Some buffers glow green because crops are well, some glow green because forests breathe fully in summer, and some become wet signatures rather than productive fields. Yet in that complexity lies the beauty of GIS and Remote Sensing: they do not merely give answers—they give patterns, and they ask us to interpret with humility.
